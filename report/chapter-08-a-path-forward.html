Chapter 08 In progress · Outline

A path forward

Constructive, non-prescriptive observations on what would help South Carolina's existing AI work connect, scale, and serve its citizens — written from a posture of championing the work already underway, not critiquing it.

In progress

This chapter is being drafted.

The recommendations below are an outline. Each will be expanded with specifics, supporting evidence, and named precedents from peer states. SCAIO welcomes input from the institutions and individuals doing the work — the goal is to amplify rather than prescribe.

Connective tissue

Recommendation 1

A standing statewide AI working group, with rotating chairs from industry, academia, and the public sector.

The pieces exist — SCRA's AI Leadership Hub, ADAPT in SC, the AI Center of Excellence, university institutes, sector groups. A lightweight standing forum that brings their leads into one room on a regular cadence would compound returns from work each is already doing.

Recommendation 2

A shared, public AI deployment registry for state and local government.

What systems are in production, in pilot, or under procurement, with which vendors, for which decisions. NIST AI RMF gives a usable template; SC could be among the first states to ship a public, browsable version.

Capacity-building

Recommendation 3

Statewide AI literacy curricula calibrated to legislators, school boards, and small-business owners.

Three different audiences, three different curricula, all aligned to the same vocabulary. SCAIO's /learn/ hub is being built to serve as the public-facing component; partnerships with the Center of Excellence and SC ETV could make this scalable.

Recommendation 4

An accelerated technical-college AI pathway, with stackable credentials.

South Carolina's Technical College System is the state's most underused AI workforce asset. Short, stackable certifications for AI-adjacent operational roles (data labeling, MLOps, model auditing, applied prompting) could fill a gap that four-year degrees take too long to address.

Infrastructure

Recommendation 5

A transparent data-center build-out framework.

Santee Cooper's 50 MW+ rate pilot is a strong start. A statewide framework that pairs energy-pricing transparency with siting clarity, water-use disclosure, and community-benefit standards would let the state attract the data-center investment it wants without the externality fights that have surfaced in other states.

Recommendation 6

Anchor commitments from large public-sector AI buyers.

State agencies, the technical college system, and the public university system collectively spend significant amounts on enterprise software each year. Coordinated procurement preferences — for vendors with transparent data practices, SC-based deployment partners, or workforce commitments — could be a powerful market signal without raising costs.

Sector-specific

Recommendation 7

An applied industrial-AI consortium anchored at Clemson and USC.

Linking the universities' AI capacity to the state's manufacturing base — BMW, Boeing, Volvo, Mercedes-Benz Vans, Michelin — through a structured industry-academic consortium could cement SC's "industrial AI capital of the Southeast" positioning.

Recommendation 8

A clinical-AI deployment standard for SC health systems.

MUSC's Center for AI and the Clemson–MUSC AI Hub already do much of the underlying work. A statewide standard for clinical-AI deployment — drawing from MUSC, Prisma Health, and rural-hospital input — would let SC lead on a topic where most states are still reactive.

Public trust

Recommendation 9

An annual SC AI Public Confidence survey.

Tracking how South Carolinians actually feel about AI in their schools, clinics, jobs, and government — by region, sector, and demographic — would be one of the highest-leverage ways to keep the policy conversation grounded.

Recommendation 10

Real, ongoing investment in independent SC-focused AI journalism and research.

The work SCAIO does is one piece of this. The Post and Courier, SC Daily Gazette, SC Public Radio, and emerging digital outlets are others. The state's information ecosystem will need more, not less, capacity for substantive AI coverage.
